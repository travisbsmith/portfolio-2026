## Beyond Pretty Pictures

Every week, I review portfolios from designers hoping to join our team. The ones that stand out aren't always the most visually polished. They're the ones that clearly demonstrate **how the designer thinks**.

Here's my framework for creating case studies that showcase your thinking.

## The STAR Framework for Design

I borrow from the classic STAR interview format, adapted for design:

- **Situation**: What was the context? Business goals, constraints, team composition.
- **Task**: What were you specifically responsible for?
- **Action**: What did you do? This is where process lives.
- **Result**: What happened? Quantify where possible.

Most case studies I see are heavy on Action (process porn) and light on Situation and Result. Balance is key.

## Show Your Constraints

Real design happens within constraints. Budget, timeline, technical limitations, organizational politicsâ€”these are the realities of the job.

Don't hide your constraints. Embrace them:

> "We had 3 weeks, one engineer, and a legacy codebase that couldn't support custom components. Here's how we delivered impact anyway."

This is more impressive than a fantasy project with unlimited resources.

## Be Honest About Your Role

Nothing frustrates hiring managers more than unclear ownership. If you were one of five designers on a project, say so. Explain specifically what you contributed.

Good: "I led the research phase and designed the onboarding flow. Worked closely with another designer who focused on the dashboard."

Bad: "We redesigned the entire product."

## Show Dead Ends

The final design is polished. But what about the seven versions that didn't work? The approaches you tried and abandoned?

Including failed attempts shows:
- You explore before committing
- You can evaluate your own work critically
- You iterate based on feedback

## Quantify Your Impact

"Improved the user experience" is meaningless. What specifically improved, and how do you know?

Strong signals:
- Conversion increased by X%
- Support tickets decreased by Y%
- Task completion time reduced from A to B minutes
- NPS improved from X to Y

If you don't have hard metrics, qualitative feedback counts:
- User research quotes
- Stakeholder testimonials
- Team feedback

## Structure for Scannability

Recruiters and hiring managers are busy. They're scanning, not reading.

Make your case studies scannable:
- Clear headlines for each section
- Key takeaways in bold or callouts
- Visual hierarchy that guides the eye
- Summary at the top for those who won't read further

## A Template to Start From

1. **Overview** (2-3 sentences): What was the project? What was your role?
2. **Context**: Business situation, goals, constraints
3. **Challenge**: The specific problem you were solving
4. **Process**: How you approached it (research, ideation, iteration)
5. **Solution**: What you shipped (with visuals)
6. **Results**: Impact and learnings
7. **Reflection**: What you'd do differently

---

Your portfolio is an artifact of your thinking. Make it easy for people to understand not just what you made, but how you approach problems.

That's what gets you hired.
