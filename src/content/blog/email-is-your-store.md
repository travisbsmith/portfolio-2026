I worked with a founder selling nasal strips — fun designs, Amazon-first business, good repeat purchase rates on her Amazon listings. She'd built a Shopify store to move toward owned distribution, and she was driving some paid traffic to it.

Her conversion rate on Shopify: around 1%. Amazon: over 5%.

This is a common gap, and the instinct is to blame the store design or the product page. But the real answer is usually simpler: Amazon has years of relationship data on those customers. It knows who they are, when they buy, what they need next. Your Shopify store doesn't have any of that — unless you've built it.

Email is how you build it.

## The three flows that run your store

Most Shopify stores need exactly three email automations running before anything else. In order of impact:

**1. Abandoned cart**

Someone added your product to their cart and didn't buy. They were interested enough to get that far — which is a lot. An abandoned cart email, sent 1-2 hours after they left, typically converts at 5-15%. On a store with meaningful traffic, this one flow often outperforms everything else combined.

Set it up in Klaviyo or Shopify Email. One email is enough to start. The subject line doesn't need to be clever — "You left something behind" works fine.

**2. Welcome series**

Someone joined your email list. This is your highest-engagement window — open rates for welcome emails are 3-4x higher than regular campaigns. Use it.

Send 3 emails over the first 5 days: (1) welcome + your brand story, (2) your hero product with social proof, (3) your best offer or a reason to buy now. This sequence converts browsers into buyers before they forget about you.

**3. Post-purchase**

A customer just bought. This is the beginning of a relationship, not the end of a transaction. Send them a thank-you that feels human, set their expectations about shipping, and introduce them to a complementary product or a reason to come back.

Post-purchase flows are also where you ask for reviews. Time it for 10-14 days after delivery. The best review request email is short, personal, and asks a genuine question.

## The list hygiene problem

Here's something I see constantly: a founder has 1,000+ "contacts" in Klaviyo and wonders why their email performance is poor. They run a campaign, get 8% open rates, assume email isn't working for them.

What's actually happening: their list is full of bots, dead emails, and people who opted in once and never engaged again. Klaviyo charges by active profile count. If you're paying for 1,000 contacts but only 100 of them are real, you're wasting money on a list that's dragging down your deliverability.

The fix is list hygiene:
1. Filter for contacts with zero email opens in the last 6 months
2. Run a re-engagement campaign to that segment
3. Delete everyone who doesn't respond
4. Repeat quarterly

A clean list of 200 engaged contacts outperforms a dirty list of 2,000 every time — both in deliverability and in sales.

## Klaviyo vs. Shopify Email: the honest take

Start with **Shopify Email** if you have under 1,000 contacts and don't need advanced segmentation. It's free for the first 10,000 emails/month, it's built into your admin, and it handles the three core automations perfectly well. No separate account, no migration headaches, no platform fees while you're figuring things out.

Switch to **Klaviyo** when you need:
- Segment-based logic ("customers who bought Product A but not Product B")
- SMS in the same platform as email
- A/B testing on flows
- More than 10,000 emails/month

Klaviyo's free plan covers up to 250 contacts, so you can get started without cost. But don't rush to Klaviyo for the sake of it — the feature set you pay for is genuinely valuable once you have the contact volume to use it.

## The channel most founders underinvest in

Paid advertising shows up in your analytics immediately. Email shows up slowly and compounds over time. That makes email feel less exciting to work on, especially in the early days when you're chasing momentum.

But email has a structural advantage that ads don't: you own the list. Meta can change its algorithm. Google can raise CPCs. TikTok can get banned. Your email list belongs to you, travels with you to any platform, and has no per-send cost after the platform fee.

The most valuable thing you can build in the first year of a DTC brand is an engaged email list of people who want to hear from you. Every dollar you spend driving traffic without capturing an email is a dollar of leverage you left on the table.
